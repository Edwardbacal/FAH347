  • Key traits borrowed from Post-Impressionism

    Adapted from Art Since 1900, 1906

  • Flat, unmodulated planes of nonmimetic color (from Gauguin and van Gogh)

  • Thick contours with a rhythm of their own (from Gauguin and van Gogh)

  • Differentiating linear marks by varying their thickness and their closeness to one another (from van Gogh)

  • Incorporating non-western stylistic visual styles and themes (from Gauguin)

  • Treating the pictorial surface as a totalizing field where everything, even the unpainted white areas, plays a constructive role in bolstering the energy of the picture (from Cézanne)

"Primitivism"

emphasis on the quotation marks

The approbation of and enthusiasm for primitive art, generally understood to mean the art of Africa and the Pacific Islands. Western artists had a particular interest in this type of ethnic art c.1905–c.1935, beginning with the Fauves, Cubists, and Die Brücke who incorporated elements of it into their own work. This in turn led to a deeper study of this subject by both anthropologists and art historians.

Clarke, Michael. "primitivism." In The Concise Oxford Dictionary of Art Terms. : Oxford University Press, 2010.

It was during the first decades of the 20th century that modernist painting, sculpture and printmaking were most responsive to the arts of Africa and the Pacific. Maurice de Vlaminck's acquisition of some African sculptures c.1905 was followed by Picasso's visit to the Musée d'Ethnographie du Trocadéro in Paris in 1907 and his subsequent assimilation of African and Pacific forms in the painting Les Demoiselles d'Avignon (1907; New York, MOMA). Members of Die Brücke, such as Emil Nolde, Ernst Ludwig Kirchner, Max Pechstein and Karl Schmidt-Rottluff, drew inspiration from art seen in ethnographic museums or, in Nolde's and Pechstein's case, on journeys to the Pacific Islands, and translated their responses into paintings, woodcuts and wood-carvings of rare starkness and vehemence. Surrealist artists were attracted to a variety of American, African and Pacific objects: Max Ernst drew inspiration from Easter Island petroglyphs, Joan Miró from masks of Arctic peoples, and Alberto Giacometti from various Polynesian sources. In the history of modernist sculpture, the achievements of Constantin Brancusi, Amedeo Modigliani, Jacob Epstein and Henry Moore, among others, may confidently be linked to non-Western antecedents, predominantly African.

While modernist ideals in the early 20th century were shaped, on the one hand, by principles of simplification, reduction to essence and organic gracefulness and, on the other, by a more agitated desire to accelerate artistic progress through an espousal of the outlandish and extravagant, it may be said that its standards of formal evolution were set by the arts of peoples labeled “primitive” or “barbaric.” Artists as diverse as Matisse and Schmidt-Rottluff found this example highly persuasive, and few avant-garde artists worked without at least one talismanic sample of what was taken to be the African or Pacific vision in the studio. Only the Futurists, for reasons of ideology, resisted the allure of African and Pacific culture.

Cardinal, Roger. "Primitivism." In The Grove Encyclopedia of American Art: Oxford University Press, 2011.

What's the effect of this painting?

The painting is unsettling - in its subject matter and its appearance, in its origins and its achievement. There is no doubt that the work marked a turning-point in Picasso's development, and that it provoked an extreme reaction in Braque, bringing the two artists into creative dialogue. On the other hand, almost nothing of the subject, scale, colour, form or mood of this painting - nothing of its ambition - survives into Picasso's Cubism proper. Les Demoiselles d'Avignon is not so much the first Cubist painting, then, as the traumatic rupture that enables Cubism to begin.

Neil Cox - Cubism

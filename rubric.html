Rubric for Journals and Essays

While evaluating and ranking your work on a numeric scale that decides your academic success in university is not exactly a perfect way of rewarding your learning or efforts, these are the criteria we're using for grading your work:

A Range (80+)

    • Analysis and interpretation

      Journal:

    • Demonstrates original and relevant observations about the material.
    • Develops upon ideas from readings and class in original ways.
    • Makes informed analyses/critiques that respond to salient points.
    • Appropriately balances the discussed texts throughout the entry and identifies relevant points of comparison and contrast.

      Essay:

    • Communicates strong understanding of a chosen topic, supported by thorough, well-selected research (e.g. art history texts, primary sources, relevant sources from outside art history)
    • Presents an original and relevant thesis, supported by informed analyses and critiques.
    • Responds to existing debates while introducing new insights.
    • Writing
    • The entry's syntax unfolds in a linear, coherent order. Ideas clearly flow from one to another.
    • Examples and quotes are carefully chosen and are included for good reason.
    • Demonstrates a personal voice that remains scholarly.
    • Formal stuff
    • Evidence of careful proofreading and revision. Nearly no writing errors.
    • Citations are done correctly, according to Chicago Style, and appear where they're supposed to.
    • Word count falls within the limits, without feeling like too much or too little has been written.
    • Formatting and assignment instructions are followed closely.

B Range (70-79)

    • Analysis and interpretation

      Journal:

    • Demonstrates observations about the material.
    • Develops, but mostly reiterates, ideas from class or the readings.
    • Makes informed analyses/critiques that respond to readings and course material, but still depends on what was said in class.
    • Balances the texts, but some points of comparison/contrast can seem strained.

      Essay:

    • Communicates good understanding of a chosen topic, supported by good research.
    • Presents a relevant thesis, supported by informed analyses and critiques.
    • Draws on existing debates but largely reiterates established debates.
    • Writing
    • The entry's syntax is linear and mostly coherent. Ideas flow from one to another but sometimes jump from one to another.
    • Good use of examples and quotes; most are there for good reason.
    • Author's voice shows personality and/or scholarly tone, but can also be generic.
    • Formal stuff
    • Evidence of proofreading and revision. Occasional typos or writing errors.
    • Citations appear, but are formatted imperfectly. Some things that should be cited are not included / things that should not be cited are.
    • Word count falls within the limits.
    • Formatting and assignment instructions are mostly followed closely.

C Range (60-69)

    • Analysis and interpretation

      Journal:

    • Few attempts to make one's own observations about the material.
    • Only reiterates ideas from class and, to a lesser extent, the readings.
    • Few attempts to respond to readings and course material or to go beyond what was said in class.
    • Little attempt to balance the texts. Points of comparison and contrast seem arbitrary.

      Essay:

    • Communicates partial understanding of a chosen topic, with inconsistent research.
    • Presents a clear thesis, but applies inconsistent analyses and critiques.
    • Draws on existing debates but only reiterates established ideas.
    • Writing
    • The entry's syntax is not linear or hard to follow. Ideas jump from one to another with minimal connection.
    • Fair use of examples and quotes: some seem arbitrary, or there are too few or too many.
    • Author's voice is largely generic, focused on summary.
    • Formal stuff
    • Little evidence of proofreading and revision. Regular typos and writing errors.
    • Citations are formatted incorrectly. Several things that should be cited are not included / things that should not be cited are.
    • Word count is off by up to 100 words (journal) or 400 words (essay).
    • Formatting and assignment instructions are followed partially.

D Range (50-59)

    • Analysis and interpretation
    • Minimal attempt to make insights and observations about the material.
    • Only reiterates ideas from class, barely touches on readings.
    • Minimal attempt to respond to the readings and course material, only reiterates what's said in class.
    • No attempt to balance texts. Points of comparison and contrast are arbitrary.
    • Communicates poor understanding of a chosen topic, with insufficient research.
    • Lacks a clear thesis or clear analyses and critiques.
    • Ignores existing debates and insights.
    • Writing
    • Ideas jump from one to another with no connection. Overall syntax of the entry is confusing.
    • Arbitrary use of examples and quotes, with noticeably few or far too many.
    • Author's voice is entirely generic, focussed on summary.
    • Formal stuff
    • No evidence of proofreading and revision. Abundant typos and writing errors.
    • Citation formatting is blatantly incorrect. Citations are applied sporadically or used unnecessarily.
    • Word count is off by up to 200 words (journal) or 800 words (essay).
    • Formatting and assignment instructions are largely ignored.

F Range (0-50)

I typically give Fs only to work that's noticeably incomplete, plagairised, or shows deliberate neglect. In any case, it shouldn't have to come to that; I'm more concerned with seeing you finish on your terms than failing your work. If you're unable to complete your work to your true ability due to exigent circumstances, please get in touch with your registrar - preferably sooner rather than later.

Journal example

Week 2: The Surreality of the Cinema

In this week's readings, Robert Desnos, Jean Goudal, Jean Vigo, and Andre Breton each reflect on cinema's relation to Surrealism, vis-a-vis the movement's aims and cultural consequences. Reading their articles together, I sense that a Surrealist impulse lends itself particularly well to cinema, or rather, exists at the heart of cinema itself. Just as the previous week's readings analyzed the cinematic medium in order to understand the reality of film form, here we have thinkers seeking cinema's underlying Surreality. To be sure, these thinkers rearticulate many notions the previous articles raise—namely, an emphasis on harnessing the cinematic image's potential— but are no longer concerned with cinema's artistic legitimacy as much as its unprecedented ability to express the unconscious.

As Goudal argues, cinema has many characteristics that express Surrealism's aim of opening art to the unexplored regions of the dream. He maintains that what is seen in the cinema corresponds exactly to a conscious hallucination, in that cinema's unique visuality draws us out of reality and immerses us in a vision of the imagination. 1 Moreover, cinema can appear illogical while still holding a “pervasive” effect on viewers, in that filmmakers can communicate their psychic experiences to viewers who can relate to them on an analogous psychic level.2 This concern with a Surrealist cine-visuality, theorized in terms of the unconscious experience of viewing recorded images, is similarly evident in Vigo's account of social cinema. Vigo defines this notion as a kind of documentary cinema that is, in distinction to conventional documentaries, rooted in the author's viewpoint. Additionally, he is interested in life caught unawares, which does not reflect the formal ordering of images as much as the psychic reality of looking and perceiving. 3 This is a “social cinema”, then, in that it reflects the unconscious mechanics that underwrite everyday social life.

Finally, we can link these concerns to Desnos' concern with eroticism, which he views as a creative force to be expressed in the cinematic image. Along with recognizing that filmgoers are desiring humans, he outlines a certain sensuousness in cinema, an eroticism of the filmic image where flesh can become more real than that of the living and through which cinema acts like a powerful cerebral drug.4

Many similarities are evident between these writers and earlier ones such as Jean Epstein, Louis Aragon, Antonin Artaud, and Germaine Dullac, who similarly theorize the aesthetics particular to the cinematic image and its visuality. Both are concerned with the subjective vision of an author, with a kind of engrossing and perceptually novel visuality, and with a poetics of the image (for example, Desnos' description of the eroticism of sensuous gestures caught by the camera recalls the notion of photogénie). These writers are also all concerned with the cinematic image's potential and/or intrinsic qualities, and perhaps even with exploring an essence of cinema. As such, considering the extent that film and psychoanalysis have been close theoretical and aesthetic bedfellows—considering that cinema is perhaps more psychoanalytically relevant than other media—I wonder if there is something deeply surreal at the heart of cinema itself. With this in mind, Breton's article, which describes his experience of aimlessly walking in and out of movie theatres, and which illustrates his attraction to cinema's seemingly unrivalled ability to effect disorientation, is perhaps ideal in conceiving the deeply surreal experience cinema offers.5 It is with cinema's intoxicating potential of conveying and realizing the disorienting non-logic of unconscious life that it expresses its surreality.

    Key traits of analytic cubism:

    First off, what is Analytic Cubism

    "Analytic Cubism (roughly 1908 to 1912) has come to refer to a process where three-dimensional objects are broken down into fragments - corresponding to their appearance from different viewpoints in space - which are then recombined in two-dimensions to produce a representation. Analytical Cubism supposedly proceeds from observed reality to art, and the near monochrome works by Braque and Picasso... especially from around 1910-11, are usually treated as classic examples of the method. Although Analytic Cubist painting rejects single-point perspective, its combination of multiple viewpoints is thought to offer a fuller version of perceived reality."

    Neil Cox, Cubism, 145

    • The colour range is reduced to an almost monochromatic palette of dark browns and/or greys.

    • Visual space is broken down and flattened out, figure look shattered.

    • The composition takes on a grid-like structure.

    • Hints of relatively-naturalistic details appear.

    Georges Braque - Violin and Palette - 1909-11

    Juan Gris - Portrait of Pablo Picasso - 1912

    What about Salon Cubism, though?

    Neil Cox identifies the following, which are based more on attitudes and ideologies than technique, and also vary considerably among artists and writers:

    1. a (progressive) dream of a new scientific era in which reality would be transformed philosophically;

    2. a (conservative) fantasy of a modern reinterpretation of the greatness of French classicism that would obliterate the artistic and cultural pretensions of all other nations and be expressed in all the arts;

    3. a desire to claim for the artist absolute power to redefine art;

    and 4. a conflicting desire to demonstrate the absolute truth of Cubism

    Cubism, 141

    Theories of Cubism

    The "conceptual" theory of Cubism, as a superior way of looking at the external world.

    For writers like Guillaume Apollinaire, Albert Gleizes and Jean Metzinger (also cubist painters themselves), Andre Salmon, and Maurice Raynal, Cubism posits "the superiority of conceptual knowledge over merely perceptual realism." "Stating that natural vision is impoverished since we can never see the whole of a three-dimensional object from any single vantage point... they argued that Cubism overcomes this handicap by breaking with a single perspective to show the sides and back simultaneously with the front, so that we apprehend the thing from everywhere, grasping it conceptually as a composite of the views we would have if we actually moved around it."

    Art Since 1900, 107

    and

    The formalist view of Cubism, as an engagement with the internal space of the picture.

    According to Daniel-Henry Kahnweiler, "Cubism was exclusively concerned with bringing about the unity of the pictorial object... the necessary fusion of two seemingly irreconcilable opposites: the depicted volumes of "real" objects and the flatness of the painter's own physical object (just as "real" as anything in the world before the artist), which is the canvas plane of the picture."

    Similarly, the American critic Clement "Greenberg saw Analytical Cubism as the increasing fusion of two types of flatness: the "depicted flatness" by which the tilted planes shoved the fragmented objects closer and closer to the surface; and the "literal flatness" of that surface itself."

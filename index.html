    • Over the last century, Cubism has stood as one of art history's best known movements, and its biggest star, Picasso, is a household name. There's no question that Cubism is crucial to the foundations of modern art, but it's worth asking what makes this movement, and its various counterparts, so compelling to eminent art historians and casual museum-goers alike? What about this eccentric, sometimes baffling style, continues to demand our attention? And what can we say about it from our current vantage point?

    To understand Cubism's place in art history, this course analyzes the social, cultural, and political contexts in which Cubism developed. Moreover, we'll follow the movements that intersect with Cubism (e.g. Italian and Russian Futurism, Orphism, Vorticism, Purism), alongside farther flung examples of its influence. What begins with a new, distinctly modern way of seeing the world unfolds into a universe of artists and writers, all working to make sense of cataclysmic shifts.

    The course is loosely organized by chronology and geography. The first half focuses on Cubism-proper, spanning the influence of post-Impressionism to the development and dissolution of Cubist painting and collage in Paris. The second half examines how Cubism's influence spreads beyond France, morphing into different styles with different ideologies. This content is a mix of greatest hits — major artists and famous works — and deeper cuts — lesser known figures from around the world. Indeed, the seeds of Cubism sprout in many different places by way of many different people. And things are all the more interesting when we step away from the stories of European men that typically dominate modernism. So, while honouring art history's established foundations, we will also pay attention to the polyphony of narratives that make up a more diverse tangle of histories.

    Course texts consist of:

    • Main readings: chapters from textbooks such as Cubism by Neil Cox, Art Since 1900 and Concepts of Modern Art. These are the "required" readings.
    • Additional readings: a mix of primary source texts and critical essays. These are not required for lectures, but you will have to read these for your assignments.
    • Extra reference: extras, just for reference.

    An effective course is just-confusing-enough: challenging but not overwhelming. Ideally, students will finish this course having grasped about three quarters of it (the other quarter is where the best learning happens). How that happens, though, is different for everyone, depending on how you learn and what you want from this course. Above all, I hope you will pursue what's meaningful to you while exercising an open mind. That said...

    My aim is for students to

    • Develop intellectual skills with which to discuss art from critical and analytical positions: to understand art's history and contexts while interpreting it from an informed, present-day perspective.
    • Nurture curiosity and creativity and to experience new connections to art and ideas. I hope this course makes you feel excited about art and excited to participate in conversations about it.

    At the end of this course, you "should" be able to do the following:
    • Perform visual and theoretical analyses of artworks.
    • Identify artworks in relation to movement, period, and style.
    • Interpret artworks in relation to their social, political, and cultural histories.
    • Understand relevant art historical debates about artists and art movements.
    • Effectively read and write art historical scholarship.

    Active engagement is essential to an effective learning experience, so, this class features discussion periods and informal activities. I intend to give space to express the diverse reactions, impressions, and sensations we experience around art.

    This assignment comprises five separate journal entries, each between 500-700 words, containing academic responses to weekly readings. Each entry should address the main and additional readings from an individual class (do not mix texts from different weeks). To help guide you, I will provide guiding questions about each class. I encourage you to refer to these, though I also encourage you to pursue your own questions.

    This assignment assesses the following skills:
    • Recognizing and concisely summarizing salient topics from course readings.
    • Identifying and interpreting connections between different texts.
    • Expressing original ideas, critiques, and analyses about readings and related artworks.
    The purpose of this assignment is for you to...
    • Carefully read (even re-read) and reflect on art historical scholarship.
    • Exercise critical, analytical, and interpretive skills in discussing art.
    • Develop critical writing skills (in this case, to explore complex ideas in a concise format).

    The first two journals are due on Quercus (not email) by Friday, February 17 and are worth 20% of your final grade.

    The next three are due on Quercus (not email) by Friday, March 31 and are worth 30% of your final grade.

    The late penalty is 1 mark per day, beginning on midnight after the due date. Late submissions will not be accepted after two weeks and will take longer to be returned.

    This assignment asks you to create a diagrammatic timeline that details a historical period relevant to Cubism; that is, not the history of Cubism itself but a chosen path (or paths) that runs through it. In addition to outlining the period's historical details (relevant events, individuals, artworks, institutions, texts, etc.) your timeline should make a larger art historical argument with its own thesis. Consider what you say about art through the way you present it — through what you choose to include and exclude and how you connect the dots.

    This timeline is more than a point-form list. At the least, you should generously illustrate it with examples and textual citations; more importantly, it's an opportunity to creatively engage with art history using different media. There is no precribed way to to make this timeline, it can be a PDF, a poster, a slide-show, a story, a website, a video, an audio-installation, a mural, a performance... I encourage you to experiment and have fun with it. You won't be graded on your creative skills, but you should be deliberate about your timeline's form, format, and media, and how those things shape your message.

    In addition to the timeline itself, you will have to provide a supporting statement (400-500 words) to explain the following:

    • The specific history your timeline illustrates and why you chose it.
    • What this timeline teaches us about the period it details - what art historical thesis does it arrive at?
    • What motivated you choice of details to include (or not include) and your choice of medium/format.
    This assignment asks you demonstrate the following skills to:
    • Synthesize cultural/social/political details into a coherent historical narrative.
    • Curate a selection of artworks and texts to illustrate a historical narrative.
    The purpose of this assignment is to have you
    • Independently identify historical tangents within the larger history of the course.
    • Explore alternate forms of academic communication and creative knowledge transfer (i.e. to not just write another essay or exam).
